the ballroom is born · harlem · 1967 → 1972

THE CATEGORY IS: A WHOLE NEW WORLD

In 1967 a Black drag queen named Crystal LaBeija placed as a runner-up in a Manhattan pageant she knew was rigged for white contestants — and, on camera, refused to be gracious about it. That walk-out is one of the most electric moments in queer film. Five years later she stopped entering other people's pageants and built her own: the House of LaBeija, 1972 — the first "house."

A house is a chosen family with a mother at its head, and it is also a team that competes. Out of that single idea grew the entire Harlem ball scene: runways, trophies, and shade, invented by the Black and Latino queer kids the other rooms kept locking out. They were not given a seat at the table, so they built the whole ballroom.

Gilbert Baker's original flag had eight stripes, and the first one was hot pink. It stood for sex.

Pink fabric was hard to source at scale, so the first stripe was the first thing cut. Then turquoise and indigo were merged to royal blue so the flag split evenly down a parade route.

The stripe that meant sex was the one that got removed. On this site, it's the first color in the palette. Stripe one, restored.
