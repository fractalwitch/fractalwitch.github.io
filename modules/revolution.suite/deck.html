Knowe Thyself

In 1538, in Strasbourg, Heinrich Vogtherr printed an anatomy sheet with paper flaps — lift the layers and the body opens, organ by organ. They're called fugitive sheets, and one of them survives photographed both ways: flaps down (Wellcome wgnq6kw5) and flaps up (apc2u9px). A Renaissance interactive, doing exactly what this card does. The motto printed on them: "Knowe thyself."

That is the deck's whole doctrine. Self-study is a research method: you cannot hand a lover a map you have never drawn. Masturbation isn't the consolation prize — it's the literature review.

one unhurried hour this week, no goal, no finish line — just cartography. take notes if you're the type. you are allowed to be the world expert in you.

The Negotiation Is Foreplay

Scripts, since nobody hands them out in school:
  • "What are you hoping happens tonight?" — the whole genre, one question.
  • "What's off the table?" — limits first; freedom lives inside the frame.
  • "Show me how you touch yourself. I'll take notes." — outsource to the expert.
  • The four magic dials: slower · softer · right there · don't stop. Grant your partner all four, out loud.

Negotiate clothed, fed, and sober-ish. Mid-scene check-ins ("still good?") are not mood-killers — competence is the mood.

The Orgasm Gap

The largest study of its kind (~52,000 adults, 2018) asked who usually orgasms during sex:
  • straight men — 95%
  • gay men — 89% · bi men — 88%
  • lesbian women — 86%
  • bi women — 66% · straight women — 65%
Read that middle line again. Lesbian women: 86. The gap is not anatomy — anatomy shows up to every pairing. The gap is a script: sex written to end at one particular orgasm. What closes it, per the same data: more oral, more clitoral attention, longer sessions, and partners who ask. Not a maneuver. A conversation.

retire the word "foreplay" for a month. there is no opening act — it's all the show.

The Clitoris Is Mostly Inside You

The visible glans is maybe a tenth of it. Aftercare Completes the Circuit

Intense play and intense sex run on adrenaline, endorphins, dopamine — and what goes up comes down. Drop is the comedown: shakes, chills, tears, or a blue mood, minutes or even a day later. It's chemistry, not weakness, and both the doer and the done-to get it. Tops drop too.

Anal, Correctly

The gentle-register summary: it works beautifully when you bring three things — lubricant, patience, and conversation — and fails painfully when you skip any one of them. Nothing numbing, ever: pain is information. Toys need flared bases. Go at the speed of the slowest body part. The plain-language anatomy is one dial away.
  • The anus does not self-lubricate. Lube is not optional and the right amount is more than you think. Silicone lasts longest; water-based for silicone toys.
  • Two sphincter rings: the outer one you control, the inner one is autonomic — it opens with warmth, breath, arousal, and time. You cannot negotiate with it. You can only befriend it.
  • Never numbing creams. Pain is the body's only telegram; don't cut the wire.
  • Start smaller than your ambition: fingers, then toys — flared bases non-negotiable (emergency rooms have a nickname for Friday night).
  • The prostate sits a few centimeters in, toward the belly — a curl of the fingers, "come here." Vulva-owners have every right to this territory too.
  • Prep, honestly: a shower and gentle water rinse is plenty for most; fiber does more than any douche. Relax the standards along with the muscles.
  • Condoms and/or PrEP for penetration with new partners; the next card holds the alphabet.

whatever you try, debrief it kindly: "keep / adjust / archive." curiosity without a report card.

The Alphabet of Staying Alive

  • PrEP — a pill (daily or on-demand) that prevents HIV about as well as anything in medicine prevents anything. If you play with new partners, ask a clinic about it. It is the party the AIDS generation never got to attend.
  • PEP — the emergency brake: started within 72 hours of an exposure, taken for 28 days. Know it exists before you need it.
  • U=U — undetectable = untransmittable. A person with HIV on effective treatment cannot pass it on through sex. Science's love letter to serodiscordant couples; say it to anyone still carrying 1987 in their chest.
  • The rhythm: testing every 3 months when you're active with new people. Most STIs are curable; all are manageable; none are a verdict on your character.
  • The clinic has seen everything, twice, before lunch. The last untreated epidemic is shame.

book the test you've been postponing. screenshot this card as the excuse. that's what it's for.

Every Body Is Nonstandard

  • Intersex people are roughly as common as redheads (~1.7%). The binary was never even anatomically true.
  • Trans bodies: the map is yours to label. Rename your parts and require the rename; hormones re-tune sensitivity (T and E both), so treat a transitioning body as a new instrument — learned, not assumed.
  • Disabled bodies have sex, want sex, and invented half of the best communication tech out of necessity — planning as foreplay, positioning as collaboration. Access is erotic competence.
  • Aging bodies: desire doesn't retire. It renegotiates — more lube, more time, more afternoon light. The renegotiation is intimacy.
  • Scarred and rebuilt bodies — post-mastectomy, post-op, post-anything: nerve maps redraw. Patient re-mapping can turn scar tissue tender in the good way.
Pleasure is not a spec sheet. It's a practice of attention, and attention fits every chassis ever made.

Consent Is Load-Bearing

A short history of an idea, written mostly by perverts:
  • SSC (1980s, leather community): Safe, Sane, Consensual — the first popular framework, invented by the kink world to govern itself.
  • RACK: Risk-Aware Consensual Kink — the honest upgrade. Nothing is perfectly "safe"; adults name the risks and choose with eyes open.
  • FRIES (Planned Parenthood): consent is Freely given, Reversible, Informed, Enthusiastic, Specific. Reversible means mid-sentence counts.
Working parts: safewords (or the traffic lights — green / yellow / red) plus a nonverbal signal for when speech is off the table (three taps, a dropped object). No consent while unconscious, incapacitated, or afraid to say no. The absence of "no" was never "yes." Yes sounds like yes — often louder.

install the traffic lights in your relationship this week — bed or no bed. "yellow" in the middle of an argument is consent tech too.

Rope, Pain & Other Edges

The gentle register: bondage and impact play are ancient, common, and fine — with homework. The rules that matter are about circulation, nerves, never leaving a restrained person alone, and warming up. The frank card names the specifics; turn the dial when ready. The one thing said plainly at every depth: breath play has no fully safe technique. None. Risk-aware means saying that out loud.
  • Rope: two fingers flat under every wrap; check hands often — tingling or cold means re-tie now. Wrists and the radial nerve are the classic injury; safety shears within reach, always. Never leave a tied person alone. Not to answer the door. Not for anything.
  • Impact: warm the skin up through the gears. Fleshy and muscled areas take it (thighs, cheeks of both kinds); avoid kidneys, spine, neck, and joints. Thud and sting are different instruments — ask which song.
  • Marks are negotiated in advance, like everything else.
  • Breath play / choking: genuinely dangerous, no exceptions, no "safe" technique exists — arterial and vagal anatomy doesn't care how careful you feel. Risk-aware kink means naming the risks that can't be engineered away.

whatever your edge is, find its community — the people who've done it for decades wrote safety docs out of love. read before you leap.

the thesis · and the exit

Uses of the Erotic

In 1978 Audre Lorde wrote the essay this whole room is secretly footnoting: "Uses of the Erotic: The Erotic as Power." Her argument: the erotic is not decoration and not distraction — it is information, a felt measure of what full aliveness is like. And once a person has that measure, they stop accepting less — in bed, at work, from the state.

Which is why every regime in this record — the vice squads, the Secretum, the censors, the Lavender Scare — went after pleasure first. Not because it's trivial. Because it's a benchmark. People who know how good things can feel are terrible subjects and excellent revolutionaries.

read the essay — it's twelve pages and it will rearrange your furniture. then notice, once this week, where you're settling.

Pleasure Activism

adrienne maree brown's 2019 book takes Lorde's baton and runs: what if justice work felt good? What if feeling good — rested, fed, touched, delighted — were treated as evidence the politics are working, instead of a guilty secret? "Pleasure is a measure of freedom."

That's the deck played out. You've held the anatomy, the consent tech, the alphabet, the history. None of it was ever just about sex. It was about the radical proposition that your one wild body is yours, that knowing it is power, and that joy — defended, shared, organized — is a political force with a better beat.
