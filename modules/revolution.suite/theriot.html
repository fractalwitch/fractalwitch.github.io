room three · 1966 – 1969

The Riot

This room has no images.
Not because we could not license them.
Because, for the nights that matter most, they do not exist.

1959 · Cooper Do-nuts, Los Angeles. Queens and hustlers pelt police with coffee cups and donuts. The record keeps a few sentences.

1965 · Dewey's, Philadelphia. Teenagers refused service for "non-conforming dress" sit down and stay. The record keeps a flyer.

then · the two nights the whole movement stands on. The record keeps almost nothing. Here is the shape of what it kept.

hole one · san francisco

Gene Compton's Cafeteria. Taylor and Turk, the Tenderloin.
Open all night — one of the only rooms in the city that would let trans women and drag queens sit down. Even there, the management called the police to clear them out.

From 1863 to 1974 it was illegal in San Francisco to appear in "a dress not belonging to his or her sex."

One August night in 1966 — three years before Stonewall — a cup of coffee went into an officer's face, and the room fought back. The month before, the young activists of Vanguard had picketed the cafeteria for banning them. The month after, the first stirrings of organized trans activism in the country began, two blocks away.

Not a single newspaper reported it.
The police records no longer exist.
It stayed unknown until 1995 — found by accident, twenty-nine years later, by historian Susan Stryker in a community archive.
No one knows which night it was.

hole two · new york

The Stonewall Inn, Christopher Street. June 28, 1969, toward 1:20 in the morning. A raid like a hundred raids before it — and then, this once, the street refused.

Marsha P. Johnson was there. Sylvia Rivera was there. Stormé DeLarverie was there. Six nights of it.

There is no film footage. There is no video.
Of the first night, one photograph survives.

NO FRAME

The photograph ran in the Daily News. It was taken by a press photographer named Joseph Ambrosini.

Years later a historian tracked down his family to ask what became of his negatives — a lifetime of them.
He had thrown his entire archive in the garbage.
He thought it was worthless.

Four photographs of participants exist from the second night, by Fred W. McDarrah. They live behind a licensing wall; we won't hotlink what isn't offered. You can see them at the NYC LGBT Historic Sites Project.

This is what erasure looks like in practice: not a villain burning files — a culture so sure some people didn't matter that nobody thought their history was worth a photograph.
