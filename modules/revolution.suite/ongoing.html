The floor was still warm when it started. The same rooms that had just learned to dance became the rooms that learned to bury people — fast, and young, and by the thousands. This is the groove that runs out toward the label, and it does not resolve. It hands you the needle instead.

1981 → · the plague years

Silence = Death

Six men in New York put four words on a poster in 1987. The government had said almost nothing for six years. The words were an accusation, and they were correct.

By the time a President said the word AIDS out loud in public, tens of thousands of Americans were already dead. The people the earlier rooms are about — the ones who marched in 1970, who danced at the Loft, who sewed the first flag — a generation of them went in a decade. ACT UP turned the grief into a tactic: die-ins on cathedral steps, ashes on the White House lawn, the loudest mourning the country had ever been made to watch.

"Silence = Death" is rendered here in type, never as the emblem itself — the poster is the collective work of the Silence=Death Project, and it is theirs. · ACT UP New York →

1985 → · the names project

Someone realized the dead were being denied funerals, and started sewing. Each panel of the AIDS Memorial Quilt is three feet by six — the size of a grave — made by the people left behind. Laid out on the National Mall it became the largest piece of community folk art on Earth, and you still cannot see all of it at once.

We do not print their names here. We don't have the right, and we won't borrow it. The empty cells are honest; the Quilt itself is where the names live.
